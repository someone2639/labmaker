Chapter 1: Polymerase Chain Reaction

The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is a novel technique that scientists use to quickly amplify a particular piece of DNA in vitro. PCR can produce billions of copies of specific portions of a DNA sample in a matter of hours. Scientists use PCR to directly clone DNA. Many times, when scientists analyze or use DNA there is not enough to work with so PCR is a convenient way to ensure that the researchers have a sufficient amount of DNA to analyze. Scientists also uncover hidden information using PCR and gel electrophoresis. For instance, PCR can produce a fingerprinting pattern from DNA found at a crime scene, diagnose genetic diseases in the womb, detect mutations, discover viral or bacterial infections, and/or determine the relationship among distinct species and help sequence DNA.

To start the PCR process, a centrifuge is used to separate the cellular organelles from the DNA.The centrifuge spins the sample in a test tube and the heavier organelles are concentrated into a pellet underneath a lighter liquid DNA solution. By putting just the solution into a test tube, scientists can make sure that only the DNA from the nucleus is being replicated. Similar to DNA replication in the cell, PCR requires nucleotide bases that will pair with the original DNA strands, replication enzymes that will add the nucleotides to the original strand, and primers that will tell the replication enzymes where to start adding nucleotides. Unlike DNA replication in the cell, however, the replication enzymes that are used in PCR must be tolerant of very high temperatures since the DNA sample is separated through heating. Taq polymerase, from the bacterium Thermus aquaticus found in hot springs, is used in PCR. Scientists will put all of these ingredients into a test tube and heat the mixture so that the original DNA strand will separate, then cool the tube so that the primers can stick to the DNA, then heat the DNA again so that the replication enzymes can start to add nucleotide base pairs from the 5’ to the 3’ end of the growing strand and extend new DNA strand. This process can be repeated up to 40 times and each time the DNA strand will be doubled in number, leaving the researchers with millions of copies of the same DNA sequence.



Figure 1. One cycle of the polymerase chain reaction (PCR). The double stranded substrate is denatured at 95°C. Primers are annealed to the single strands at 50°C, and extended at 72°C to produce two copies of the original template (nationaldiagnostics.com)

Chapter 2: Restriction Enzymes

To prepare the DNA made during PCR for gel electrophoresis, scientists use restriction enzymes. Restriction enzymes will scan the DNA until they recognize a particular nucleotide sequence and cut the DNA at that site, called a restriction site. Restriction enzymes are found in bacteria and archaea and serve as a defense mechanism against viruses. Different enzymes will cut DNA at certain nucleotide sequences, leading to different sized strands of DNA either with blunt ends or with ends that have an overhang of nucleotides, known as “sticky” ends.



Figure 2 Well-known restriction enzymes, their origin, and the DNA restriction sites that they cleave (Andres, D and Usma, A. 2013. Restriction Enzymes and PCR)

Chapter 3: Gel Electrophoresis

By using gel electrophoresis, scientists can compare different samples of DNA that have been amplified by PCR and cut by restriction enzymes. Gel electrophoresis creates a map that separates the cut DNA fragments according to their size. The first step in gel electrophoresis is to create a gel from a polysaccharide called agarose. Agarose usually comes as a powder, but will dissolve when heated in a buffer solution and subsequently into a gel when it is cooled. A comb is inserted into one end of the gel before it solidifies to create wells where the different DNA samples will be placed. Since microscopic pores exist between agarose molecules, DNA can flow through the gel. During gel electrophoresis, an electrical current is run through the Agarose gel with a negative anode by the wells and a positive cathode at the other end of the gel. A buffer solution is used to surround the gel and provide ions that carry a current (usually Tris/Acetate/EDTA (TAE). Since the backbone of DNA is made up of negatively charged phosphates, the DNA will be attracted to the positive cathode and move through the gel towards the positive end.

One well (either on the very left or very right side of the gel) is reserved for a DNA ladder, segments of DNA of known sizes that are used to compare the sizes of DNA fragments from samples in the other wells. After the current is turned on, the DNA ladder and the DNA fragments from the samples will flow through the gel and separate by size, with the smaller fragments being able to travel longer distances and larger fragments travelling shorter distances. The resultant “map” will have fragments from each sample, as well as the DNA ladder, separated out by size with the shorter fragments by positive cathode and the larger fragments by the wells and negative anode. The DNA is stained with a binding dye (ethidium bromide) that inserts itself into the spaces between the base pairs of the DNA. Ethidium bromide fluoresces, allowing the stained DNA fragments to glow when placed under UV light. Each glowing mark on the gel is called a “band,” which contains a large number of DNA fragments of the same size.



Figure 3 Overview of gel electrophoresis with gel box and resultant gel "map."
(www.britannica.com/science/gel-electrophoresis)

As mentioned previously, gel electrophoresis can be used for a variety of applications. For example, scientists may use gel electrophoresis to determine if a sample of DNA is abnormal. Imagine that a mutated strand of DNA is cut by a certain restriction enzyme that does not cut the “healthy” DNA strand. These two samples would be of different lengths. If the scientists have a sample of DNA that is known to have the mutagen, another sample that is known to be healthy, and a final sample that they are unsure of, they can test all three samples to check if the unknown sample contains the mutagen or not.



Figure 4 Example of a gel electrophoresis map to determine if DNA of an unknown sample is mutated.

Gel electrophoresis is also used widely in forensics. Since different people have different DNA, forensic scientists can take DNA found at a crime scene, amplify it using PCR, and compare it to the victim and suspects of a crime, in order to determine who was at that crime scene. If the DNA found at the scene of the crime matches the DNA of one of the suspects it is highly likely that that suspect was present at the scene.



Figure 5 Results of gel electrophoresis for forensic investigation (fdrsummerdrugs.wikispaces.com)

Gel electrophoresis can also be used in paternity tests and to compare genetics of different species. In addition, scientists use gel electrophoresis to analyze the products of PCR, without using restriction enzymes to make sure that the DNA clones are indeed all the same and that no DNA was mutated during the PCR process. Though it was presented here that gel electrophoresis is a process that occurs after PCR, gel electrophoresis can be used as a preparative technique prior to PCR, cloning or DNA sequencing. For instance, if you know the particular size fragment of DNA that you want to clone, you can remove that fragment from the gel and then run it through the PCR process.

Overall, PCR, the use of restriction enzymes, and gel electrophoresis have all served and continue to function as important processes in terms of biotechnology. There has been much progress in the field of biotechnology in such a short period of time and the advancements in this field have not slowed. For example, biotechnology is driving advancement of medicine by allowing doctors to determine disease risk based on genetics. In addition, biotechnology is allowing the medical field to make headway towards personalized medicine. A new discipline known as pharmacogenomics can potentially determine how a patient’s genetics will affect his or her response to medicines. These biotechnological techniques have proven to be useful not just in medicine, but in all fields of science and, as they progress, will only help to advance society as a whole.
